Cross Contamination

Welcome to the Cross Contamination page of Wheatless Warfare! Cross contamination occurs when gluten-containing foods come into contact with gluten-free items, potentially leading to harmful exposure for those with celiac disease or gluten intolerance. Even trace amounts of gluten can trigger serious reactions, making it crucial to understand how this can happen in your kitchen, at restaurants, or anywhere food is prepared. Here, you'll find detailed insights into common sources of cross contamination and practical strategies to prevent it. By staying informed and vigilant, you can protect your health and enjoy safe, delicious meals. Let's ensure your gluten-free journey remains worry-free!

What is Cross Contamination?

I found that one of the hardest parts about becoming completely gluten-free was navigating the world of cross contamination. What even is it? How was I supposed to avoid it? And most of all, I clean my stuff all the time!! How was I still getting contaminated?! After visiting a dietition who gave me a lot of advice (highly recommend) and resources I was able to define some key elements of cross contamination.

Cross Contamination
Cross-contact (also known as cross contamination) is a process by which a gluten-free product comes into contact with something that is not gluten-free. Cross-contact of gluten-free food with gluten-containing food can occur at any time. This includes from the field, to the processing plant, to the store or restaurant, and to your kitchen. It can occur anywhere food is stored, prepared, and served. [source1]
"May Contain" on Food Labels
May Contain means some manufacturers voluntarily include a separate advisory statement, such as may contain or produced in a facility, on their labels when there is a chance that a food allergen could be present. A manufacturer might use the same equipment to make different products. Even after cleaning this equipment, a small amount of an allergen (such as peanuts) that was used to make one product (such as cookies) may become part of another product (such as crackers). In this case, the cracker label might state “may contain peanuts.” [source2]
Restaurant Requirements
Don't be afraid to demand safety! Restaurants are required to adapt to your needs if they provide gluten-free options by the FDA. FDA recognizes that compliance with the gluten-free regulation in processed foods and food served in restaurants is important for the health of people with celiac disease. FDA’s regulation established a federal definition of the term “gluten-free” for food manufacturers that voluntarily label FDA-regulated foods as “gluten-free.” The definition was intended to provide a reliable way for people with celiac disease to avoid gluten, and, given the public health significance of gluten, restaurants’ use of gluten-free labeling should be consistent with the federal definition. [source3]
